**Growing Upward (#46)**
*earth over wind · the receptive · pushing upward*

Within the earth, wood grows. That's the image — literally, a tree pushing through soil from below. Not a sudden eruption. Not a dramatic arrival. Just steady, patient, underground work that eventually breaks the surface and keeps going. The image is an instruction: this is how real growth happens. Not by force from above, but by accumulation from below.

*Pushing Upward has supreme success. One must see the great man. Fear not. Departure toward the south brings good fortune.*

The judgment is almost irritatingly confident. Supreme success — not conditional, not "if you do everything right." Just: this is the direction, and it works. The great man isn't a person with a title. It's the version of yourself that's already further along, the one who knows what you're just beginning to learn. Departure toward the south means moving toward clarity, toward warmth, toward what's visible. The fear is that you're not ready. The hexagram says you've been ready longer than you think, you just haven't started moving yet.

This hexagram names a time of accumulation. Not the kind where you hoard resources or wait for permission. The kind where you do the small thing every day until the small thing becomes a structure. The wood doesn't ask the earth if it can grow. It just grows. The earth receives it, supports it, doesn't get in the way. That's the posture here: keep pushing upward, and let the ground do what ground does.

## the changing lines

**The third line** — *Going up into an empty city.*

This is the line that catches most people off guard. You push upward expecting resistance, and instead you find nothing. No walls, no guards, no opposition. Just empty space where you thought something would block you. The warning is subtle: don't mistake the absence of obstacles for a trap. Sometimes the city is empty because nobody's defending it because nobody thought you'd get this far. Keep going. Don't stop to look for the fight that isn't there.

**The fourth line** — *The king offers him Mount Ch'i. Good fortune.*

Mount Ch'i was the ancestral home of the Chou dynasty — sacred ground, the place where legitimacy lived. The king doesn't give you a reward. He gives you a place to stand. This line is about being recognized not for what you've done, but for what you now represent. You've grown enough that your presence changes the landscape. Accept the ground you're given. It's not a test.

**The top line** — *Going up in darkness. Ceaseless perseverance brings good fortune.*

This is the last push, and it happens without full visibility. You can't see the summit, but you can feel the angle of the climb. The darkness isn't punishment — it's the condition of being near the top, where the light hasn't reached yet because you're still rising toward it. The instruction is to keep going without needing to see the outcome. Not blind faith. Just the muscle memory of all the steps that brought you here.

## the relating hexagram

**Hexagram 24: Return / The Turning Point (復)** — *thunder over earth*

The primary hexagram grows upward. The relating hexagram returns. This is the arc: you push upward until you reach a height where the only direction that makes sense is back toward the beginning — but not the same beginning. A return to origin with everything you've gained. The movement isn't linear. It's a spiral. You climb, you turn, you climb again from a higher ground.

Return is the oldest magic in the book. The winter solstice hexagram. Light coming back after the longest night. What this means for today: the growth you're doing now will eventually circle back to something you thought you'd left behind. A person, a place, a version of yourself. You won't be going back to fix it. You'll be going back to complete it.

## synthesis

The Five of Wands this morning set the tone: friction. Not the destructive kind, the clarifying kind. You're in a space where multiple forces are competing — your own impulses, other people's expectations, the version of events you've been telling yourself. The wands are crossed, and nobody's backing down. But the hexagram says the conflict isn't the point. It's the friction that makes the upward movement possible. Wood doesn't grow through packed earth without resistance. The resistance is what gives the growth its shape.

The hexagram sees what the tarot sees: growth happening below the surface, invisible to everyone including you most of the time. The changing lines confirm it. Line 3 says the empty city is real, not a trick. Line 4 says you'll be given ground to stand on. Line 6 says you'll finish the climb in the dark, and that's fine.

What this means practically: you don't need to figure out the whole trajectory today. You need to do the next small thing that pushes upward. Send the email. Have the conversation. Make the thing. The hexagram calls it heaping up small things to achieve something high and great. That's not poetry. It's a work instruction.

The relating hexagram says you'll come back around to something you thought was finished. Don't be surprised when the return shows up. It's not regression. It's the spiral completing its first full turn.
