**Eradicating (#21)**

Thunder and lightning. The image is violent and precise — a crack of sound, a bolt of light, then the thing that was stuck is split open. This is not a gentle hexagram. It describes a moment where something has been lodged in the system so long that the only way forward is to bite through it. Not nibble. Not negotiate. Bite.

*Biting Through has success. It is favorable to let justice be administered.*

The judgment is clear about method: this is not a time for mercy or delay. The thing that needs to happen involves a decision that will feel harsh to someone. Maybe to you. But the hexagram insists that success lives on the other side of that bite. The "justice" here isn't courtroom justice — it's the justice of a situation finally being named for what it is. A truth that everyone has been circling finally landing on the table.

This is the hexagram of the decisive break. The clean cut. The conversation you've been dreading that will actually, once it's over, make everything simpler. The hexagram sees that you've been holding something in your teeth for too long, afraid to close your jaw. It says: close it. The bite is the release.

## the changing lines

**Line 3** — *Bites on old dried meat and strikes on something poisonous. Slight humiliation. No blame.*

This is the line of getting what you asked for and immediately regretting it. You bite into the situation — finally, you act — and the thing you were trying to resolve turns out to have a toxic core you didn't see coming. Old dried meat: this is not a fresh wound. This is something that's been sitting for a while, and the longer it sat, the more complicated it got. The "poison" is the surprise. You thought you knew what you were dealing with. You didn't.

But the line says *no blame*. The humiliation is slight, temporary, and not your fault. You couldn't have known. The point isn't that you made a mistake. The point is that you bit anyway. That's the posture this line rewards — the willingness to act even when the situation turns out to be messier than expected. The poison doesn't kill you. It just makes you wish you'd picked something easier.

**Line 4** — *Bites on dried gristly meat. Receives metal arrows. It furthers one to be mindful of difficulties and to be persevering. Good fortune.*

This is the line that follows the third. After the poison, after the humiliation, you keep going. The gristle is harder than the dried meat — it doesn't give way easily. But the metal arrows are a gift. In the old reading, metal arrows were a reward from the ruler, a sign of favor and authority. You're being given the tools you need to finish what you started. The arrows are precision. They're the ability to aim, to strike exactly where the bite needs to land.

The line warns you to stay mindful. The difficulties aren't over. But it promises good fortune if you persevere. This is the moment where the bite becomes a process rather than a single event. You don't get to do it once and be done. You get to keep working at it until the gristle gives way.

Together, lines 3 and 4 tell a story: you act, you hit something unexpected, you feel stupid for a minute, and then you keep going with sharper tools. The humiliation was the price of entry. The arrows are what you get on the other side.

## the relating hexagram

**Adorning (#22) — 贲**

When the changing lines complete their movement, Eradicating becomes Adorning. This is the arc: first you break the thing open, then you arrange the pieces into something that makes sense. Grace. Beauty. The small, careful work of making a mess into a composition.

The judgment for Adorning says: *In small matters, it is favorable to undertake something.* Not the big gestures. Not the grand declaration. The small matters. The way you place a cup on the table. The word you choose instead of the other word. The thing you fix because it would bother you if you didn't.

This is where the day is going. You're not staying in the violence of the break. You're passing through it to the other side, where the work becomes about ornament, arrangement, the quiet dignity of putting things back together in a way that honors what just happened. The bite was the cost. The adornment is the reward.

## the sky

**Mercury in Cancer (12th house)** — the mind turns inward, processing old material through the lens of feeling rather than logic, and the 12th house makes it feel like you're trying to see through fog.

**Venus in Taurus (5th house)** — pleasure is available, but it requires slowness and refusal to rush; the 5th house says play is the point, not the reward for finishing your work.

**Saturn retrograde in Pisces (9th house)** — the structures you built your worldview on are being tested by something that feels like dissolution, and the test is whether you can hold your center without needing to be right.

## synthesis

The three of cups came up this morning, and it's worth sitting with that for a second. The three of cups is celebration, friendship, the toast after the work is done. It's the glass raised in the direction of people who saw you through. But it's also a card that can show up as a warning — don't skip to the party before the work is finished. Don't drink before the bite.

The Hierophant is the structure you're biting through now — the old rule, the inherited pattern, the thing you've been following because you were told to. And the Queen of Pentacles is the Adorning hexagram in human form: someone who knows how to make a life out of what's left after the break.

Here's what this means practically: something needs to be said or done today that you've been avoiding because it feels cruel or awkward or too final. The hexagram says do it anyway. The changing lines say it will be messier than you expect, and you'll feel stupid for a moment, and then you'll get the tools you need to finish. The relating hexagram says you'll be glad you did, because on the other side of the bite is something you actually want to live inside.

Bite. Then arrange. Then raise the glass.
