**Travelling (#56)** 
*fire, mountain, the clinging*

The image of the hexagram describes fire on the mountain — a beacon in a place of elevation, but also a signal of danger, as fire on a mountain can quickly spread and engulf everything in its path. This image instructs us to be cautious and aware of our surroundings, even as we strive to illuminate our path forward. 

The judgment of the hexagram says: *"The Wanderer. Success through smallness. Perseverance brings good fortune to the wanderer."* This message is not about grand, sweeping actions, but about the small, consistent steps one takes while traveling through unfamiliar or challenging terrain. It's a reminder that success in such times is not about being grandiose or trying to control everything, but about being humble, persevering, and taking things one step at a time.

The hexagram itself, Travelling, names a time of movement and transition, where one is not in their usual place of comfort or familiarity. It demands a posture of caution and reserve, not giving oneself airs or being overbearing, especially when one has no large circle of acquaintances to rely on. The hexagram sees the situation as one where the wanderer must protect themselves from evil by being obliging toward others and winning success through their humility and perseverance.

## the sky
**Mars in Gemini** (3rd house) — the drive for communication and variety is heightened, potentially leading to restlessness or a scattering of energies if not channeled productively.
**Venus in Cancer** (4th house) — emotional connections and values are deeply rooted in family and home, influencing how one seeks comfort and security.
**Saturn in Pisces** (12th house) — the need for spiritual discipline and responsibility in the face of uncertainty or chaos is emphasized, suggesting that one's spiritual practices or connection to something greater than themselves needs nurturing.

## synthesis
The single card, Nine of Swords, set the morning's tone with a sense of isolation or being trapped in one's thoughts, possibly indicating a need to break free from mental prisons or self-imposed limitations. The three-card spread showed a psychological arc from the past's Queen of Pentacles (reversed), indicating a possible disconnection from nurturing energies or a blockage in self-care, to the present's King of Wands, suggesting a current state of action, passion, or adventure, and then to the future's King of Cups (reversed), which may indicate a blockage or reversal in emotional expression or connection.

The hexagram, Travelling, sees the same situation from a structural layer, emphasizing the need for humility, perseverance, and caution in unfamiliar or transitional times. It complicates the tarot's narrative by adding a layer of movement and the necessity of navigating through the unknown with grace and smallness. The tarot and the I Ching confirm each other in the sense that they both point to a time of transition and the need for careful navigation, whether it's through the mental landscape (Nine of Swords) or the external journey (Travelling).

Practically, this means adopting a posture of humility and awareness, recognizing that success in current endeavors will come from perseverance and a willingness to take small, consistent steps rather than grand actions. It's about being mindful of one's surroundings, whether internal or external, and moving forward with a sense of caution and respect for the unknown. The insight here is to embrace the journey, not just the destination, and to find strength in humility and smallness, rather than trying to impose one's will forcefully.
