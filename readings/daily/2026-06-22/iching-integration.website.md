**Great Strength (#34)**

*thunder over heaven · the arousing over the creative · power that has not yet learned restraint*

The image is almost violent in its clarity: thunder in heaven above. Not thunder from within the clouds, not distant rumble — thunder *above* heaven, which in the cosmology of the I Ching means the power has exceeded its container. The superior man does not tread upon paths that do not accord with established order. That's not a command to follow rules. It's a warning that when you have this much force available, the only thing that can stop you is yourself — and you'd better set those limits before something else does.

*The power of the great. Perseverance furthers.*

The judgment sounds simple. It isn't. Perseverance here doesn't mean grinding through — it means knowing when to advance and when to pull back, and having the discipline to do either. Great Strength is not the strength of a boulder. It's the strength of a tide that knows exactly when to break.

This hexagram names a time when you have real power. Not potential power, not someday power — actual, measurable capacity to move things. The question isn't whether you can. The question is whether you should, and whether you can stop once you've started.

## the changing lines

**The second line** — *Perseverance brings good fortune.* This is the quiet one. In a hexagram full of momentum and force, line two is the place that says: stay put. Not out of fear. Out of knowing that the most powerful move right now is the one you don't make. The line's position is central, grounded, modest — it's the axle, not the wheel. Whatever is asking you to act, check if it's asking because it needs you to or because it knows you can. Those are not the same thing.

## the relating hexagram

**Abundance (#55)**

When the second line changes, Great Strength becomes Abundance. This is where the energy is going. Not exhaustion. Not collapse. Fullness.

Abundance's judgment is a strange comfort: *Be not sad. Be like the sun at midday.* The king attains abundance — but the king is also the one who has to hold it without letting it spill. The movement from Great Strength to Abundance is the movement from raw capacity to actual fruition. You don't have to make everything happen today. You have to let the strength settle into the shape of what's already ripening.

Let's be honest about what reversed cards are doing here. Temperance reversed isn't an absence of moderation — it's an active refusal of it. The Hanged Man reversed isn't "not suspended" — it's having gotten down from the cross before the work was finished. And the Two of Cups reversed in past position means something didn't balance. Someone gave more, or someone took more, and the scale is still tipped.

This is where the hexagram enters as the structural layer underneath. Great Strength says: you have the power to correct the imbalance. You have the capacity to walk away from whatever the Hanged Man was trying to teach you. You have the force to drink without temperance. But having the power to do something and having the wisdom to do it are two different things.

The second line of the hexagram is the key. *Perseverance brings good fortune.* Not action. Not force. Not correction. Perseverance — which in this context means the ability to stay where you are and let the situation reveal its next move. The relating hexagram is Abundance, and Abundance comes from not forcing the harvest.

What the tarot is showing you in reverse and the hexagram is showing you in forward motion is the same shape: you're at a threshold where your strength is real but your aim is off. The reversed cards are the consequences of moving too fast or holding too tight. Great Strength is the reminder that you don't have to abandon your power — you have to aim it better.

The Saturn transit in Aries is relevant here. Eighth house Saturn in Aries means the structures around shared resources, debts, inheritances, joint ventures — they're demanding discipline at exactly the moment your Aries impulse wants to burn them down. Don't. Not yet. The second line says stay put. The relating hexagram says abundance is coming if you don't break the container before it's full.

Practically: today is not a day for grand gestures or final decisions. It's a day for holding the force you have and letting it settle. The reversed Temperance card isn't asking you to become moderate. It's asking you to stop pretending you're moderate when you're not. Great Strength doesn't need to apologize for being strong. It needs to learn when to show it and when to keep it in reserve.

You have the power. That's not the question. The question is whether you can trust that not using it is also a form of strength.
