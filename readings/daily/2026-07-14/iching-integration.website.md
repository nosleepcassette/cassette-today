**Great Harvest (#14)**  
Fire in Heaven above — the clinging, the bright, the radiant — suspended over the creative, the strong, the horse. That's the image: flame held aloft, illuminating everything. Not a bonfire you stand next to, but a sun you live under. The superior man curbs evil and furthers good, thereby obeying the benevolent will of heaven. Which is a polite way of saying: when you have this much, you have to decide what to do with it.

The judgment lands flat and declarative: *Possession in great measure. Supreme success.* No asterisks, no fine print. But the fine print lives in the lines.

This hexagram names a time of abundance — not just material, but positional. You've got the resources, the leverage, the visibility. The question isn't *can I get more?* The question is *what am I holding, and who am I holding it for?* Great Harvest is a power position, but power without a container becomes fire without a hearth — it burns everything, including you.

The third line moves first: *A prince offers it to the Son of Heaven. A petty man cannot do this.* The prince — someone with rank, with something to give — offers his harvest upward, to the rightful authority. Not hoarding, not trading, not using it to buy loyalty. Offering. The petty man can't do this because the petty man sees the harvest as his alone. He can't let go of the grain. Line 3 says: if you have plenty, give some of it to the thing you serve. Not charity — alignment.

Then line 4: *He makes a difference between himself and his neighbor. No blame.* This is the other side of generosity: discernment. You can't give to everyone equally, and you shouldn't. There's no blame in saying *this person is close, that person is not.* Boundaries are not cruelty. In a time of abundance, the risk isn't scarcity — it's dilution. You can afford to be clear about who's in your circle and who isn't. In fact, you must be.

Together, these two lines describe the posture of Great Harvest: give upward, differentiate sideways. Don't let the harvest turn you into a storehouse. Don't let your generosity turn you into a doormat.

The relating hexagram is **Decreasing (#41)** — the destination. When the changing lines complete their movement, Great Harvest becomes Decreasing. The arc is clear: you can't stay in full possession forever. Something has to diminish. The question is whether you choose what decreases, or let circumstance decide for you. Decreasing combined with sincerity brings about supreme good fortune — but it requires you to let something go. The primary hexagram says *you have it all.* The relating hexagram says *now give some of it away.* Not loss. Release.

## synthesis

You woke up wanting something. Not a thing — a *longing*. The Knight of Cups doesn't know what he's after half the time, but he knows he's after it. That's the tone.

That's the surface story. The hexagram is the structural layer underneath.

Great Harvest says you have more than you think. Not more *stuff* — more capacity. More leverage. More of what the Magician reversed was supposed to channel. The Knight of Cups is riding into a field that's already full of grain, but he's looking at the horizon, not the ground. He's chasing the next feeling instead of harvesting what's ripe. That's the disconnect.

The reversed Nine of Cups confirms it: you got the wish, but you didn't know how to receive it. The cup was full, but you were looking for a different cup. Great Harvest says *stop looking. This is it. Now offer it upward and differentiate sideways.* Give some of it to the thing you serve — your actual work, your people, your purpose. And make clear who's in and who's out. The Knight of Cups wants to give his cup to everyone. The hexagram says no — give it to the Son of Heaven. The one who deserves it.

The Ten of Swords future isn't a prophecy of doom. It's the natural consequence of holding a full cup wrong. If you keep chasing, keep misplacing your attention, the sword comes. But the hexagram offers a different path: curb evil, further good. That's a practical instruction. Look at what you're carrying right now — the harvest, the resources, the attention you have — and ask: *is this serving the benevolent will of heaven, or is it serving my restlessness?*

The relating hexagram, Decreasing, is the landing. Something has to shrink. Maybe it's the fantasy you've been nursing (the Knight's vague longing). Maybe it's the wish you already got and didn't enjoy (the reversed Nine of Cups). Maybe it's the identity you built around the Magician's power. Decreasing asks you to choose what goes. If you don't, the Ten of Swords will choose for you.

So here's the posture: you have a lot. You feel like you're missing something. You're not. The fire is in heaven — the abundance is real. The Knight of Cups is not wrong to want; he just needs to aim. Offer what you have to the right authority. Make the distinctions you've been avoiding. Let something decrease so the rest can breathe.

That's the day. Not a fix. A direction.
