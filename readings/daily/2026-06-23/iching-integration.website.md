**Replenishing (#48)** — water over wood / the abysmal over the gentle

The image is almost unbearably literal: a wooden bucket descending into dark water. Water over wood. The well has been dug. The structure is there. What matters now is whether the bucket actually reaches the water and whether the jug comes back intact. The image doesn't ask you to build anything new. It asks you to finish what you started.

*The town may be changed, but the well cannot be changed. It neither decreases nor increases.*

You cannot exhaust this source. That's the first thing. Whatever you're drawing from — your skill, your patience, your capacity to care — it isn't running low because you used it yesterday. The well doesn't work that way. What runs low is your willingness to keep lowering the bucket. What breaks is the jug, not the water.

The judgment names two specific failure modes: the rope that doesn't go all the way, and the jug that breaks. Both are about stopping short. The rope that almost reaches — that's hesitation at the last moment, the refusal to commit fully to the draw. The broken jug — that's carelessness with what you've already received, spilling it before it can be used. The well is not the problem. The problem is how you handle the descent and the return.

This hexagram names a time of maintenance, not creation. The superior man encourages people at their work and exhorts them to help one another. Not because the work is glorious, but because it's necessary. Someone has to tend the well. Someone has to make sure the rope is long enough, the bucket is sound, the water stays clean. These are not heroic acts. They are the acts that make everything else possible.

## the changing lines

**Line 4** — *The well is being lined. No blame.*

This is the only line moving, and it's a quiet one. The well is being lined with stone or brick. Nothing dramatic. No water gushing forth, no discovery of a hidden spring. Just reinforcement. Structural care. Someone is making sure the walls don't crumble, that the well stays clean and usable.

No blame means exactly what it says: there is nothing to apologize for. The work of lining the well is not flashy. It doesn't produce results you can show anyone. But it prevents collapse. It keeps the water potable. It's the kind of maintenance that only looks unnecessary to people who have never had a well cave in on them.

Today, this line asks what you're reinforcing. What structure in your life needs its walls shored up before you can draw from it again? Could be a relationship, a creative practice, a financial system, a daily rhythm. Could be your own nervous system. The well-lining doesn't make the news. It makes the water drinkable.

## the relating hexagram

**Great Exceeding (#28)** — lake over wood / the joyous over the gentle

When the fourth line completes its movement, Replenishing becomes Great Exceeding. And the image shifts from a well being maintained to a ridgepole sagging under too much weight. The threshold is buckling. The structure is at its limit.

*Preponderance of the Great. The ridgepole sags to the breaking point. It furthers one to have somewhere to go.*

This is where the day is heading. Not toward disaster, but toward a reckoning with capacity. The well-lining was preparation. Great Exceeding is the moment when you discover whether the preparation was enough. Something is about to press against the edges of what you can hold. Not maliciously — just the weight of accumulated life.

The key phrase is *it furthers one to have somewhere to go.* Movement is the answer. Not hunkering down, not bracing. Going somewhere. The sagging ridgepole will not hold forever, but you don't need it to. You need to move through the door before it buckles.

Primary to relating: you maintain the well so that when the weight comes, you have clean water to drink. You line the walls so the structure doesn't collapse under the pressure. The arc is from care to capacity. From tending to carrying.

The Queen of Cups reversed is the jug that breaks. It's the emotion that spills before it reaches anyone. It's the capacity to hold feeling that has cracked under too much use. The well-lining in line 4 is the repair the reversed Queen of Cups is asking for. You cannot pour from a broken vessel. You cannot draw water with a cracked bucket. The maintenance has to come before the pouring.

The Hanged Man reversed says you've been resisting the pause that would let you see the well needs lining. The Queen of Swords says you now see it clearly. The Queen of Cups reversed says the vessel is already showing stress fractures. The Three of Cups says the water is still good and the people are still there.

Hexagram 48 says: line the well. Not tomorrow. Not when it's convenient. Now. The rope is long enough. The water is deep enough. The only question is whether you'll do the unglamorous work of making sure the bucket holds.

Great Exceeding says the weight is coming. But you have somewhere to go. And the well will still be there when you come back.
