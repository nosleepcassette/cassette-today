**Dispersing (#59)**
_Wind over Water_, _the gentle_

The image of Dispersing describes the wind driving over the water — a force that disperses and scatters, yet does so gently, without destruction. This image instructs us to consider how our actions and decisions can have a dispersing effect, breaking up stagnation and bringing movement to areas that have become still. The wind over water is a potent symbol of change that is both powerful and serene.

The judgment of Dispersing says: "_Dispersion. Success. The king approaches his temple. It furthers one to cross the great water. Perseverance furthers._" On the surface, this judgment seems to promise success through the act of dispersing or spreading out. However, it's also talking about a kind of success that comes from approaching the inner temple — the place of personal truth and wisdom. Crossing the great water symbolizes a significant transition or journey, one that requires perseverance. The success here is not just about achieving a goal, but about the journey itself, the process of growth and transformation.

Dispersing, as a hexagram, names a time of transition and change. It demands a posture of openness and flexibility, a willingness to let go of what has become stagnant or stuck. This hexagram sees the situation as one that requires movement and the dispersal of energies that have become too concentrated. It's a call to action, but an action that is gentle and considered, rather than forceful or aggressive. The situation is ripe for change, and the question is how we will approach this change — with resistance or with the gentle perseverance suggested by the judgment.

The tarot and the I Ching see the same situation from different angles, confirming each other in the need for movement and change. The tarot emphasizes the psychological and emotional aspects of this change, while the I Ching provides a broader, more philosophical context, emphasizing the natural and gentle nature of this dispersal. The tarot complicates the I Ching's message by suggesting that this change is not just about external circumstances, but also about internal states and emotional readiness.

Practically, this means that the posture to carry into the day is one of gentle openness and a willingness to let go. It's about embracing change not as a force to be feared, but as a natural part of growth and transformation. The action, or non-action, is to approach this change with the perseverance and clarity suggested by the hexagram, allowing the dispersal of stagnant energies to bring about a new state of being. This is not a call to drastic action, but to a mindful and gentle engagement with the currents of change that are already in motion.
