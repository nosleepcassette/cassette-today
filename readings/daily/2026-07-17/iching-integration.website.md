**Travelling (#56)** — Fire over Mountain. Travelling, FIRE, MOUNTAIN, THE CLINGING.

Fire on the mountain — that's the image. A small flame flickering on a high peak, visible from far away but with nothing to anchor it. The wanderer's fire: bright, temporary, self-contained. The judgment says: *Success through smallness. Perseverance brings good fortune to the wanderer.* This isn't a hexagram about vacation. It's about being out of place — voluntarily or not. You don't have the usual supports: no deep roots, no long history, no network that knows your name. The instruction is to stay small, stay obliging, don't overreach. The fire on the mountain can't pretend to be a forest fire. It just has to burn cleanly where it is.

The second line: "The wanderer comes to an inn. He has his property with him. He wins the steadfastness of a young servant." This is the good one. You find shelter, you keep what's yours, and you earn loyalty through decency. Not through power or money — through being the kind of person someone wants to serve. Today, that means accepting help when it's offered, and not mistaking independence for isolation. The inn is temporary, but the young servant's loyalty is real. You don't need to own the place to be cared for.

The sixth line: "The bird's nest burns up." This is the bad one. The wanderer who gets too comfortable, who forgets they're a guest. The nest burns because the fire that kept you warm now consumes your shelter. The warning: don't mistake a temporary arrangement for a permanent home. Don't get attached to the inn as if it were yours. Laugh too loud, stay too long, and the whole thing goes up. The sixth line is the price of forgetting you're passing through.

When these lines change, the hexagram becomes 32 — Duration (Long Lasting). The wanderer's journey, if navigated with care, leads to something that lasts. Not a fixed place — duration is a process, not a destination. The question the primary hexagram is asking: can you move through the world lightly enough that the movement itself becomes your stability? The relating hexagram answers: yes, but only if you keep the fire small and the nest provisional.

## synthesis

The single card this morning is the Six of Cups. Sweet, nostalgic, a little wistful. You're carrying something from before — a memory, a kindness someone gave you, a version of yourself that still matters. The hexagram says travel light, but the Six of Cups says you're allowed to keep some things. The past isn't baggage if it's a gift you're carrying for someone else. The wanderer's property includes what he's earned and what he's been given. Don't leave it behind just because it's old.

The three-card spread shows the psychological arc underneath all of this. Past: Two of Swords — a decision you've been refusing to make, a blindfold you've kept on because seeing would mean moving. Present: Knight of Swords — you've finally broken through. You're moving fast, maybe too fast, cutting through whatever was blocking you. Future: The Hanged Man — you're about to stop. Not because you failed, but because the Knight's momentum will slam you into a perspective you can't outrun. The Hanged Man is the inn from the second line: you hang, you see the room upside down, you realize you were never going to arrive — you were only ever passing through.

The hexagram names the structural layer: you're a wanderer in all of this. The Knight thinks he's conquering; the wanderer knows he's just passing through. The Two of Swords was the refusal to admit you were already on the road. The Hanged Man isn't a disaster — it's the sixth line's warning, softened. The nest might burn, but you'll be hanging from a different branch by then.

What to carry into today: stay small. Accept the inn when it appears. Don't mistake the Knight's velocity for progress. And if the nest burns, don't rebuild it — just walk to the next ridge. The fire on the mountain doesn't need a home. It just needs to burn.
